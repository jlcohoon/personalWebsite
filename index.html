About

I research other scientists and how they do their work.

I study change in scientific practice.

After earning my bachelors in Cognitive Science at the University of Virginia, I began work at the Center for Open Science (COS). There, while working on the Reproducibility Project: Psychology, I became curious about the ways in which technology and academic culture affect scientists' work. From there, I moved on to the PhD program at UT Austin's School of Information under the mentorship of James Howison. Given this interdisciplinary home, my research is influenced by a variety of fields such as science and technology studies, organization studies, usability research, genre theory, and computer supported cooperative work. Through work at COS and with my advisor as well as research assistant positions in psychology labs at UVA, I have conducted research using qualitative methods such as content analysis and grounded theory as well as quantitative methods using experimental design and surveys.

I remain interested in open science as an example of ongoing change in science and plan to continue studying the forces affecting academic research. In my work I examine how knowledge and research products move and change between minds, labs, hard drives, and journals as well as how those moves might recursively shape the knowledge and products themselves. If scientific research is meant to be a paragon of trustworthiness and unbiased reporting, I ask: What do researchers do to pursue that goal? How does their infrastructure affect their outcomes? And, are do we really all agree on what science is meant to be?
