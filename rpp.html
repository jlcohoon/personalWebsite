Estimating the Reproducibility of Psychological Research

The Project:

After earning my bachelors in Cognitive Science at the University of Virginia, I began work at the Center for Open Science (COS). There, while working on the Reproducibility Project: Psychology, I became curious about the ways in which technology and academic culture affect scientists' work. From there, I moved on to the PhD program at UT Austin's School of Information under the mentorship of James Howison. Given this interdisciplinary home, my research is influenced by a variety of fields such as science and technology studies, organization studies, usability research, genre theory, computer supported cooperative work, and anthropology. Through work at COS and with my advisor as well as research assistant positions in psychology labs at UVA, I have conducted research using qualitative methods such as content analysis and grounded theory as well as quantitative methods using experimental design and surveys.

My Role:

I remain interested in open science as an example of ongoing change in science and plan to continue studying the forces affecting academic research. In my work I examine how knowledge and research products move and change between minds, labs, hard drives, and journals as well as how those moves might recursively shape the knowledge and products themselves. If, as Woolgar and Latour suggest, we might view science as myth, I ask: How are those myths constructed? How can that process be changed? How does myth become infrastructure?

Outside of professional life, I am a craft dilettante, single-speed cyclist, and patio gardener. My husband, Chris, and I live with our dog, Zula, and cat, Fred, in an increasingly plant filled home in Austin, TX. Always on the lookout for elevation change, I love to travel to find a good trail to explore or mountain to climb.

While I usually go by Hannah (a nickname given to me by my palindrome loving father) my legal name, under which I publish, is Johanna Cohoon.
